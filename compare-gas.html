Different Business Gas that Suppliers Offer

When conducting a gas tariff comparison, it's important to understand the various types of business gas tariffs available. The key types to consider are as follows.

  • Fixed-rate: A fixed-rate tariff means you pay the same price for your gas over a specific time, like 12 to 18 months. Even though the price is fixed, your total bill can still change depending on how much gas you use. Once this time is up, your supplier might switch you to a regular rate. But you can also choose to find a new fixed-rate deal, which could save you money.
  • Variable-rate tariffs: A standard variable rate (SVT) tariff is like a flexible plan where the gas price can change with the market. After your fixed-rate deal ends, you'll probably switch to this, but it could mean higher costs due to current high gas prices. There's a price cap rule from Ofgem to limit how much suppliers can charge on these plans, aiming to protect customers from steep price hikes.
Causes of Increased Business Gas

If your business is grappling with higher gas bills, there are several reasons for this situation. .

One primary factor is the changeable wholesale gas prices, which rise and fall due to global supply, demand, geopolitical events, and weather conditions. When these prices go up, energy providers often pass the increased costs on to businesses.

Maintenance and improvement of gas supply infrastructure, including pipelines and storage facilities, can result in higher prices as safety and reliability investments are made. Stricter environmental regulations and carbon reduction goals require business energy suppliers in the UK to invest in cleaner energy sources and emissions-reduction technologies, further contributing to an increase in gas rates.

Government taxes and levies, aimed at supporting renewable energy and infrastructure development, can also add to overall gas expenses. The cost of transporting gas from production sites to end-users can vary based on distance and infrastructure, impacting businesses located farther from gas sources.

Furthermore, market competition and short-term supply and demand fluctuations can result in variable tariffs. Economic factors, such as inflation and currency exchange rates, can affect the cost of imported natural gas, thus influencing gas tariffs.

Ways to Cut Extra Expenses on Your Business Gas Bill?

Boosting your business's gas efficiency is a smart investment for the long haul. To lower your gas bill, start by using energy-efficient equipment, insulating your workplace, and keeping your heating system well-maintained. Smart thermostats can help you save as well. Make sure to seal any drafts, consider getting an energy audit, and negotiate better gas contracts.

Keeping an eye on your gas usage, educating your employees about energy-saving practices, and exploring renewable energy sources can all contribute to savings. You can also benefit from lower rates during off-peak hours and check for any tax incentives available.

Comparing your gas usage to industry benchmarks can help you identify areas for improvement.

These steps will not only reduce your gas expenses but also make your business more environmentally responsible.

How to Switch Business Gas Suppliers?

Start by researching different business gas suppliers, either online or through a broker, considering factors like different business energy contracts , pricing, and customer feedback. Ensure your current contract is eligible for switching by reviewing its terms, including the notice period, contract end date, and exit fees. Avoid switching prematurely if you're in a fixed-term contract that hasn't reached its end date to avoid penalties.

If you're within your current contract's notice period, inform your current supplier of your intent to switch in writing. Once you've decided on a new supplier, request a formal quote outlining the terms of the new contract, such as pricing and contract duration. Review the quote and, if satisfied, electronically sign the new contract after understanding its terms and conditions.

Your new supplier will manage the switch, ensuring no interruptions in your gas supply. A final meter reading will be arranged with both old and new suppliers to settle outstanding balances. After the switch is complete, your new supplier will confirm the change in writing, including details of your new contract. Monitor your bills to ensure they reflect the new terms, and regularly review your contract as it approaches its end date to secure the best deal for your business. Carefully assess the terms and conditions of your new contract to ensure they meet your business's requirements. If you encounter questions or concerns, don't hesitate to contact your current or new supplier for clarification and assistance.
